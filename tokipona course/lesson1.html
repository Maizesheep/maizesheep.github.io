the basics

If you're here, I assume you know what toki pona is and how it's a minimalistic conlang published by Canadian linguist Sonja Lang in 2001. If you didn't already know, please find out more from the toki pona website tokipona.org



First, let's start with phonology. These are the sounds in a language. There are 14 sounds in toki pona, and, conveniently, 14 letters!
These letters are exactly like in English: m, n, s, w
The other consonants aren't too difficult, you just need to get yourself used to them. p, t, k are not as strongly pronounced as they are in English (they're unaspirated as linguists would call them). Basically, pronounce them without the puff of air and you'll be golden.
The l, unlike in most dialects (with a notable exception being Irish dialects), is a light l. What does this mean? It means that you need to get rid of that knot in the back of your throat when you say your typical English l sound.
The j is the one that is most likely to trip you up and have people misunderstanding you if you mispronounce it. The j simply sounds like a y at the beginning of words does in English, not like the j in judge or juice or jazz. Treat it like a y, it'll make your life easier.
